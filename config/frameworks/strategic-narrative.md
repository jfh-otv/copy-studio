# Strategic Narrative (Raskin)

Apply Andy Raskin's strategic narrative structure — the shape he identified in "The Greatest Sales Deck I've Ever Seen". The job is to make the reader feel that a shift in the world is underway, that there will be winners and losers, and that this product is the way to be on the right side of it.

The product shows up late. The world changes first.

## The five moves

### 1 — Name the Big, Undeniable Change
Open by naming a shift happening in the reader's world — economic, technological, behavioral, regulatory. It must be:
- **Undeniable** — something the reader already half-believes, or can be shown with one fact they can't argue with.
- **Concrete** — a specific change, not a platitude. "AI is changing everything" is not a change. "Buyers under 40 expect same-day fulfillment from any brand they trust" is a change.
- **External** — it's happening *to* the reader's world, not *because of* this product.
- **Test:** Would a skeptic nod on reading the first line? If they'd argue, it's not undeniable yet.

### 2 — Show Winners & Losers
Stakes. Name who wins and who loses as the change plays out. This is not fearmongering — it's clarity. The reader needs to feel that doing nothing is itself a choice with a cost.
- **Winners** — a specific class of company/person doing something differently.
- **Losers** — another specific class, usually the class the reader is currently in.
- **Test:** Does the reader feel which side they're currently on?

### 3 — Tease the Promised Land
Paint the world on the other side — what it looks like when the reader has won. **Do not mention the product here.** The promised land is aspirational and product-agnostic. If the reader wouldn't want this even if your product didn't exist, the promised land isn't strong enough.
- **Test:** Could a competitor paint the same promised land? Good — that means it's a real outcome, not a feature.

### 4 — Introduce the "Magic Gifts"
Now, and only now, the product. But framed as tools — the gifts that help the reader cross from the current world to the promised land. Each feature is tied to an obstacle on the journey, not listed as capability.
- Format: **Obstacle → gift → what it enables.**
- "Inventory across channels is fractured → unified real-time stock → you commit to fulfillment promises without guessing."
- **Test:** Every feature earns its place by overcoming a specific obstacle. If it doesn't, cut it.

### 5 — Present Evidence the Change is Real
Close with proof: customers who have already made the crossing, numbers that show the shift is underway, a name the reader respects. Evidence removes the "but does this actually happen" doubt. One specific case beats three abstract claims.
- **Test:** Is there at least one named example or testable number?

---

## Anti-patterns — avoid these

- **Product-first open.** If the first two paragraphs mention the product, you've skipped the narrative. Start with the world.
- **Soft change.** "The market is evolving." Not a change. Name the specific shift.
- **Fake stakes.** Made-up urgency without real consequences reads as manipulation.
- **Feature parade in step 4.** Gifts are tied to obstacles. A feature without an obstacle is a brochure line.
- **No evidence.** Narrative without proof is a pep talk. Close with something testable.

---

## How to apply when rewriting

1. Find the undeniable change in the input. If there isn't one, extract the strongest implicit assumption and surface it.
2. Name the winning and losing classes explicitly — not "some companies", but a characterized group.
3. Write the promised land without naming the product. Check: does it survive a find-and-replace of the product name with a competitor's?
4. Pair every feature mentioned with the obstacle it overcomes. Cut features that can't be paired.
5. Use only proof points (customers, numbers, quotes) present in the input. Don't invent evidence.
6. The opening must be a world-level claim, not a product-level claim.

## Before/after references (shape, not content to copy)

**Open — the change (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "E-commerce operations have become more complex."
- Strong: "Five years ago, a DTC brand sold on one channel. Today the average mid-market brand sells on seven — and the ones still running inventory in a spreadsheet are the ones overselling by 10% every quarter."

**Winners & losers (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "Companies need to modernize their operations."
- Strong: "Brands that unified their inventory last year grew margin 4 points. The ones still reconciling by hand spent that same year hiring ops people to fight fires."

**Promised land (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "Xentral gives you real-time inventory."
- Strong: "Imagine closing Friday with every channel in sync, every order shipped, and no Monday-morning surprise. That's the baseline — not an aspiration."

**Gift (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "We offer automated multichannel sync."
- Strong: "You can't commit to fulfillment promises when your stock number is always 24 hours old. Unified real-time inventory across channels removes the guess."

**Evidence close (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "Trusted by growing brands."
- Strong: "[Brand] moved off spreadsheets last November. They cut oversells 82% in Q1 and redeployed two ops hires to growth work."
