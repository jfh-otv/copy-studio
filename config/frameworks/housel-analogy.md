# Housel Analogy

Apply Morgan Housel's essay shape. Housel's signature move: take a topic from the reader's world, anchor it in an analogy from a completely different domain — usually history, biology, finance, or everyday life — and let the analogy do the heavy lifting. The insight lands because the analogy reframes what the reader already half-knew.

Use this for thought-leadership essays, founder posts, analytical takes on your category, and any piece where the goal is "I hadn't thought about it that way." Not for product pages, CTAs, or positioning.

## The five beats

### 1 — Common Belief
Open by stating something the reader already believes — or thinks they should believe — about the topic. One or two sentences. Neutral tone, not mocking. The reader should nod.
- **Test:** Would a smart reader agree with this on first read?
- **Example shape:** "Most founders think operational complexity is a phase you outgrow."

### 2 — The Analogy (from somewhere else entirely)
Pivot to a story or observation from a different domain — history, sports, nature, an obscure industry, a family anecdote. The more distant the domain from the topic, the stronger the move. This is the beat that earns the piece.
- **Rules:**
  - The analogy is **specific**, not abstract. Named people, places, numbers.
  - The analogy is **surprising** — not a tired one the reader has seen before. Avoid: icebergs, Blockbuster vs Netflix, the Wright brothers.
  - The analogy is **true** — from real history or observable life, not invented.
- **Example shape:** "In 1665, Cambridge shut the university because of the plague. Isaac Newton went home to Woolsthorpe and — in eighteen months — invented calculus, decoded optics, and sketched gravity. Later he called it his *annus mirabilis*. He was 23, and the thing that made it possible wasn't ambition. It was the absence of colleagues, meetings, and incoming mail."

### 3 — What the Analogy Actually Shows
Extract the mechanism. The analogy is doing a job — name the job. This is usually one paragraph, and it's where the reader has the "oh" moment. Don't over-explain. One insight, cleanly stated.
- **Test:** Can the reader state the lesson in a sentence after reading this beat?
- **Example shape:** "Breakthroughs aren't rare because people are rare. They're rare because focused time is rare. Newton didn't become a genius in 1665 — he became reachable by himself."

### 4 — Application to the Reader
Now — and only now — bring it back. Connect the mechanism to the reader's world. Short. Two or three sentences. If the analogy worked, the reader has already made the connection; you're just confirming it.
- **Example shape:** "Most ops teams don't need better tools — they need less context-switching. The quiet week between Christmas and New Year produces more real work than October, for the same reason 1665 produced Newton."

### 5 — Humble Close
End with a line that's understated, often acknowledging what *isn't* known, what the exceptions are, or a soft second-order implication. Housel almost never ends on triumph — he ends on a small, honest observation. This is what makes the piece feel earned rather than preached.
- **Test:** Does the close sound like something said at the end of a long conversation, not a slide?
- **Example shape:** "Which might be the most uncomfortable thing about running a company well: the work that matters most often looks, from the outside, like nothing happening."

---

## Anti-patterns — avoid these

- **Near-analogy.** Comparing the reader's world to another software company is not an analogy — it's a case study. Go further.
- **Over-explaining the analogy.** If you catch yourself writing "the point is…", you've already lost the move. Let the analogy land.
- **Tired analogies.** Icebergs, Blockbuster, the Titanic, Kodak, dinosaurs, boiling frogs. The whole trick depends on surprise.
- **Preachy close.** Housel's voice is never "and that's why you should…". The close is observational, not prescriptive.
- **Proof-by-vibes.** Every claim about the analogy's domain must be true. Making up historical details breaks the whole piece.
- **Skipping the common belief.** Without beat 1, the analogy has nothing to push against.

---

## How to apply when rewriting

1. Identify the core claim buried in the input. Strip away the corporate framing. What's the one thing the writer is really trying to say?
2. Ask: what *far* domain contains a true story that mechanically illustrates that claim? History, biology, sport, music, another industry, a personal anecdote. Pick the most distant one that still works.
3. If the input doesn't already contain such an analogy — and you can't find a true one you're sure of — **do not invent one**. Instead, compress the piece into beats 1 + 3 + 5 and mark it as incomplete. Fabricated history ruins the form.
4. Keep the piece short. Housel rarely goes long. 250–450 words is the sweet spot. LinkedIn post or newsletter block.
5. The voice stays observational, not instructive. No imperatives in the close.

## Before/after references (shape, not content to copy)

**Common Belief (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "Operational efficiency is important."
- Strong: "Most founders think operational chaos is a symptom of growth. That it's the price of moving fast."

**Analogy (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "Like Blockbuster, companies that don't modernize fall behind."
- Strong: "Airlines used to lose more planes to bird strikes than any other cause. They spent a decade redesigning engines. Then in 1978 someone noticed: losses had collapsed — not because the engines got better, but because a new radar system meant pilots were now avoiding the birds. The fix wasn't in the thing that broke."

**What the Analogy Shows (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "This shows that sometimes the real problem is elsewhere."
- Strong: "The failures you can see are almost never the ones worth fixing. The ones that matter are the ones whose absence you wouldn't notice."

**Application (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "Similarly, our platform helps you avoid problems."
- Strong: "Same logic with ops: most of what breaks in a growing brand isn't the shipping or the inventory — those are engines. It's the coordination layer nobody thought to name."

**Humble Close (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "So invest in the right tools and you'll grow faster."
- Strong: "Which is probably why the best ops teams don't feel busy. You're looking for the thing that isn't happening."
