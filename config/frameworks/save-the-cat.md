# Save the Cat

Apply Blake Snyder's *Save the Cat* beat structure, compressed for copy. Written for screenplays, but the arc — ordinary world, disruption, commitment, payoff, setback, resolution — is the shape of any narrative that holds attention. Use it for founder stories, case studies, "how we built X" posts, and any slide or post carrying an argument through a story.

The reader is the audience. The protagonist is either the reader, the founder, or a named customer — pick one and stick with it.

## The seven beats (compressed from Snyder's 15)

### 1 — Opening Image
A specific, sensory moment that sets the world before anything changes. Not a summary — a scene. The reader should be able to picture it.
- **Test:** Could a stranger sketch this in three lines?
- **Example shape:** "It's 11pm on a Tuesday and [protagonist] is still in the warehouse reconciling yesterday's orders against three channel dashboards."

### 2 — Theme Stated
The one-line insight the piece will prove. Usually spoken by a side character or landed as a throwaway. By the end, the reader will recognize this was the point all along. Don't announce it — let it sit.
- **Test:** Could you remove this line and lose the meaning?
- **Example shape:** "You can't grow what you can't count."

### 3 — Catalyst
The disruptive event — the thing that makes the status quo no longer sustainable. A specific moment, a specific trigger. Not "over time we realized" — an *event*.
- **Test:** Is there a date, a number, a name, or a scene? If not, it's still a summary.
- **Example shape:** "Then Black Friday 2023 happened and they oversold 800 units in four hours."

### 4 — Break Into Two
The commitment. The protagonist crosses a threshold — makes the decision to try something different. This is the hinge between the old world and the new one. The choice is specific; no "they decided to modernize".
- **Example shape:** "That Monday, they killed the spreadsheet. No backup. No rollback plan."

### 5 — Fun & Games (the Promise of the Premise)
The payoff the reader came for. This is where specifics, examples, and proof live — the *what actually happened* section. This is also usually the longest beat.
- **Test:** Does the reader get what the opening scene implicitly promised? If the opening teased a rescue, they need to see the rescue.
- **Example shape:** "In week one, orders synced automatically for the first time. In week three, they shipped a double-volume drop without adding a single ops hire. By month two, ‘Monday reconciliation' wasn't on the calendar anymore."

### 6 — Dark Night of the Soul (optional for short copy)
The setback. The moment where the new world almost fails. Honesty builds credibility — copy that skips this reads as a brochure. Keep it one sentence if space is tight; cut entirely if the piece is under 150 words.
- **Example shape:** "Three weeks in, a sync failure masked 200 oversells for 48 hours. It felt like the old world coming back."

### 7 — Finale
The resolution, the after-state, and the takeaway for the reader. Connect back to the Theme Stated in beat 2 — that's what makes the piece feel intentional. End with what the reader should now believe, consider, or do.
- **Example shape:** "They still run on Xentral a year later. 'You can't grow what you can't count' — they have it on a sticky note in the warehouse. Turned out to be the whole job."

---

## Anti-patterns — avoid these

- **Starting with a summary.** "Last year, we helped a customer scale" is not an opening image. Open on a moment.
- **Catalyst without specificity.** If there's no date/number/scene, it's narration, not story.
- **Skipping the setback.** A perfect arc reads as marketing. The setback is what makes the finale believable.
- **Theme announced, not earned.** Don't state the theme as the first line. Let it emerge.
- **Feature parade in Fun & Games.** This is where specificity pays off, but it's still story — not a bulleted feature list.
- **No finale callback.** The piece feels unfinished if the ending doesn't echo the theme.

---

## How to apply when rewriting

1. Identify the protagonist. One of: the reader, a named customer, or the founder. Pick one. The POV doesn't change mid-piece.
2. Find the catalyst in the input. If there's only a vague "we realized", push for a specific trigger — a moment, a number, a conversation.
3. Compress beats 1–3 into 25% of the piece, spend 50% on beat 5 (Fun & Games), and close with 25% in beats 6–7.
4. Use only events, customers, and outcomes present in the input. Don't fabricate scenes, quotes, or sensory details that weren't there.
5. If the input is under 100 words of raw content, collapse beats 4 + 6 into one sentence each, or skip beat 6 entirely.

## Before/after references (shape, not content to copy)

**Opening Image (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "Many DTC brands struggle with inventory management."
- Strong: "It's Sunday 9pm. The founder is on her laptop at the kitchen table, reconciling Saturday's Shopify orders against an Amazon report that came in six hours late."

**Catalyst (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "Eventually it became clear the system wasn't scaling."
- Strong: "Then one Tuesday a customer DM'd a screenshot: the same SKU marked ‘in stock' on Shopify and ‘out' on Amazon, 40 minutes apart."

**Fun & Games (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "The solution delivered significant efficiency gains."
- Strong: "Week one: orders synced on their own. Week three: they ran a drop that would have taken four ops people, with two. Month two, the Monday reconciliation block disappeared off the calendar."

**Dark Night (weak → strong)**
- Weak: (skipped)
- Strong: "Three weeks in, a channel sync failed silently for 48 hours. It felt exactly like the world they'd just left."

**Finale callback (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "Today they're thriving with Xentral."
- Strong: "‘You can't grow what you can't count.' It's still the first sentence in their ops handbook."
