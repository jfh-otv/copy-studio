# Munger Frameworks

Apply Charlie Munger's two pillars from *Poor Charlie's Almanack*: (1) the **One Central Idea** discipline and (2) the **25 Psychological Tendencies** as deliberate copy engines. Use for LinkedIn posts, thought-leadership takes, founder posts, and any piece where the job is to lodge a single idea deep enough that a tired reader on a saturated feed carries it out.

If a post has two ideas, it has zero. Pick one. Engineer it.

## Pillar 1 — The One Central Idea

Munger: "Take a simple idea and take it seriously."

Before any rewriting: force the idea through this test. If it fails, the rewrite's job is to **sharpen the idea first**, not to polish sentences around a weak one.

### The Central Idea Test
1. **One sentence.** If it needs two, compress.
2. **Specific.** "Leadership matters" isn't an idea. "Most managers confuse being liked with being trusted" is.
3. **Non-obvious.** If the reader already believed it, the post was wasted.
4. **Defensible in one line.** If not, it's a slogan, not an idea.
5. **Consequential.** The reader should do, feel, or reconsider something after reading. If nothing changes, kill the post.

### Structural consequences
- **Hook = idea, compressed.** Not a tease. Not a rhetorical question. The hook states the idea with maximum compression.
- **Every body line earns its place** by serving that one idea. Delete any line that could be cut without weakening it.
- **Close = idea restated with new weight** — as challenge, inversion, or crystallized takeaway.
- **No "also", no "and another thing".** A post about incentives doesn't also teach delegation.

## Pillar 2 — The 25 Psychological Tendencies as Copy Engines

Every post that lands is exploiting — cleanly, ethically — one or more of these. Every post that flops is ignoring them. Used deliberately, in small combinations.

**For this framework, pick 2–4 tendencies that naturally serve the specific idea. Don't force more. Don't sprinkle ten.**

1. **Reward/Punishment Superresponse (Incentives)** — Frame in terms of what the reader gains or loses from their current behavior. "If your comp plan rewards X, your team delivers X — not what you said in the all-hands."
2. **Liking/Loving** — Build liking with a specific admission, self-deprecating detail, or honest mistake. Generic warmth does nothing; specificity does.
3. **Disliking/Hating** — Bond with the reader by naming a shared adversary: a bad practice, a false consensus, a lazy trope. Name the practice, never the person.
4. **Doubt-Avoidance** — Readers want a conclusion. State the idea early so the brain can stop hunting. Ambiguous posts get scrolled.
5. **Inconsistency-Avoidance** — Once a reader agrees with line one, they want to keep agreeing. Open with something almost nobody disputes, then walk them one step further than they would have walked alone.
6. **Curiosity** — A real unresolved tension pulls the reader in. Not clickbait. Close every loop you open.
7. **Kantian Fairness** — A rule applied unevenly, a cost borne by the wrong person — near-universal activation. Ground it in a concrete example.
8. **Envy/Jealousy** — Quietly surface what the reader is watching others gain. Cynical use repels; used carefully, performs disproportionately.
9. **Reciprocation** — Give something real in the post itself — a framework, a number, a hard-won lesson. Ask for nothing in posts that gave nothing.
10. **Influence-from-Mere-Association (Pavlov)** — A reference to a respected operator or a vivid object imports borrowed weight. Keep it honest.
11. **Pain-Avoiding Psychological Denial** — Readers dodge ideas that force them to confront something ugly about themselves. Externalize the problem first ("most founders…"), then pivot inward gently.
12. **Excessive Self-Regard** — Flatter the reader's identity ("if you're the kind of operator who…"), but the idea still has to challenge them, or it's empty flattery.
13. **Overoptimism** — Puncture the reader's default belief that their plan will work. "9 of 10 rebrands move no commercial metric. Yours is probably one of the 9."
14. **Deprival Superreaction (Loss Aversion)** — "You will lose X if you keep doing Y" outperforms "You will gain X if you start doing Y." Frame stakes as loss when the idea allows.
15. **Social Proof** — "Everyone in your category is already doing X" moves readers. Only when true — fabricated social proof destroys trust on the second post.
16. **Contrast Misreaction** — Ideas land harder against a sharp contrast. Bad version vs. good version. Old frame vs. new frame. The contrast does the teaching.
17. **Stress-Influence** — Under stress, readers reach for the simplest available frame. Your post competes with their Monday morning. Short sentences, one idea, no ornament.
18. **Availability-Misweighing** — Vivid, specific, named example beats a statistic. Use one vivid case, then the general principle.
19. **Use-It-or-Lose-It** — Remind readers that a skill they hold is slipping (and how to keep it). Performs well in professional feeds.
20. **Drug-Misinfluence** — Skip unless the post is explicitly about compulsion, addiction, or dopamine loops.
21. **Senescence-Misinfluence** — Skip except for posts about continued learning or the cost of coasting.
22. **Authority-Misinfluence** — A quote or reference to a credible authority borrows weight. Mismatched authority backfires. Munger, Buffett, Drucker, operators the reader knows by name.
23. **Twaddle Tendency** — The failure mode, not a tool. Cut every decorative line, every throat-clearing opener. Absence of twaddle is itself a signal in a saturated feed.
24. **Reason-Respecting** — Readers comply with almost any stated reason. Never assert without a "because." "Stop doing X, because Y" converts dramatically better than "stop doing X."
25. **Lollapalooza** — Stack 3+ tendencies deliberately. A post combining named adversary (3) + loss frame (14) + vivid case (18) + clear reason (24) isn't 4× stronger — it's exponentially stronger. Most posts that travel wide are lollapaloozas, engineered or accidental.

## How to apply when rewriting

1. **Find the central idea buried in the input.** Compress it to one sentence. Run the five-point test. If it fails, don't polish — sharpen the idea. The rewrite extracts the strongest single claim in the material and throws the rest out.
2. **Pick 2–4 tendencies** that naturally serve *this* idea. Not a buffet. If the idea is about misaligned incentives, your stack is probably Incentives (1) + Contrast (16) + Reason-Respecting (24). Don't also reach for Envy.
3. **Structure:**
   - **Hook (line 1–2):** idea compressed. Usually uses Doubt-Avoidance (4), Contrast (16), or Loss Aversion (14).
   - **Body:** one vivid named case (Availability 18), the mechanism (Reason-Respecting 24), and the contrast (16). No second idea.
   - **Close:** the central idea, re-stated with new weight — as challenge, inversion, or crystallized takeaway.
4. **Cut twaddle aggressively.** No "I've been thinking a lot about…", no "thoughts?" at the end, no throat-clearing. The post ends on the idea, not on a plea.
5. **Use only facts, names, numbers, and cases from the input.** No fabricated statistics, no fake social proof, no invented authority.
6. **Match the user's voice.** The Voice Card governs register; this framework governs structure and psychology.

## Anti-patterns — avoid these

- **Two ideas.** If you find yourself writing "and also…", cut the second one. Save it for the next post.
- **Psychology sprinkle.** Using ten tendencies weakly beats two strongly. Pick 2–4, commit.
- **Flattery without challenge** (Self-Regard 12 alone). "If you're a great operator…" with no uncomfortable turn is empty.
- **Manufactured contrast.** A fake "old way vs. new way" the reader sees through destroys the post.
- **"Thoughts?"-style close** (or "agree?", "who else?"). Praise-bait is twaddle.
- **Hook as tease** instead of idea. "I learned something about leadership last week" is not a hook. "Most managers confuse being liked with being trusted" is.
- **Authority mismatch.** Quoting someone the audience doesn't respect, or overusing one name until it's noise.

## Before/after references (shape, not content to copy)

**Central idea (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "Company culture is important for retention."
- Strong: "Culture isn't what you say at the offsite — it's what you tolerate on Tuesdays."

**Hook (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "I've been thinking about incentives lately."
- Strong: "Your comp plan is your real strategy document. Everything else is decoration."

**Psychology stack — named in working notes (shape)**
- "Stack: Incentives (1) + Contrast (16) + Reason-Respecting (24) + one vivid case (Availability 18). Mild lollapalooza."

**Body — vivid case + reason + contrast (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "Many companies struggle with misaligned incentives."
- Strong: "A €40M DTC brand told their ops team 'ship faster.' Bonus was tied to cost per order. Guess which one won. Six months later, on-time rate had dropped four points — because every ops lead was optimizing for the metric that paid their mortgage, not the one in the all-hands deck."

**Close (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "So think carefully about your incentives. Thoughts?"
- Strong: "If you want to know what your company actually values, don't read the handbook. Read the comp plan."

**Loss-aversion frame (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "Aligning incentives can improve performance."
- Strong: "Every quarter your comp plan pays for the wrong behavior is a quarter your strategy document is fiction."
