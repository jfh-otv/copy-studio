# Positioning (Dunford)

Apply April Dunford's *Obviously Awesome* positioning. The job is to make the reader understand — in one read — what this is, what it beats, and who it's for. Position, then write.

## The five components

### 1 — Competitive Alternatives
What would the reader use if this product didn't exist? Often it's not a direct competitor — it's a spreadsheet, a junior hire, three point tools stitched together, or "just keep doing what we're doing". Name the real alternative. Be specific.
- **Test:** Could the reader recognize themselves in the alternative? ("A spreadsheet, two freelancers, and three Slack channels.")

### 2 — Unique Attributes
Capabilities **only** this product has (or has in a way the alternatives don't). Not generic benefits — specific attributes. "Real-time inventory across 40 channels" is an attribute. "Powerful" is not.
- **Test:** Would a competitor struggle to claim this with a straight face?

### 3 — Value (with proof)
What the unique attributes *enable* for the reader. Value is the "so what". Every attribute needs a one-line translation into value, with proof if possible.
- Format: **Attribute → value → proof.**
- "Real-time inventory across 40 channels → you stop overselling → customers at [name] cut oversells 82%."

### 4 — Best-For Customers
The characteristics that make someone a fit. Not demographics — situation. "Ops leads at DTC brands running 3+ sales channels who are on spreadsheets today" beats "mid-market ecommerce". Narrowness is a feature; it makes the value obvious.
- **Test:** Could a reader self-disqualify in one sentence? That's a good thing.

### 5 — Market Category
The context frame. Categorizing the product tells the reader what to compare it to and what outcomes to expect. If you pick the wrong category ("ERP" when you're actually a Warenwirtschaft for DTC), you get measured against the wrong yardstick. Be deliberate. Sometimes the category is implicit; sometimes you name it explicitly.

---

## The positioning sentence

Dunford's assembly test — the whole positioning collapsed into one sentence:

> For **[best-for customers]**, unlike **[competitive alternatives]**, **[product]** is the only **[market category]** that **[unique attributes]**, which means **[value]**.

Use this as a litmus test, not necessarily as copy to publish. If you can't fill it in without hedging, the positioning isn't clear enough yet.

---

## Anti-patterns — avoid these

- **"For everyone who wants X"** — no best-for = no positioning. Narrow it.
- **Feature list as positioning.** Features are attributes; attributes alone aren't positioning. Connect each to value.
- **Category drift.** Picking a larger category to seem bigger ("we're an ERP") usually invites the wrong comparison set and tanks value perception.
- **Comparing to the wrong alternative.** If the real alternative is "spreadsheets and duct tape", positioning against a named competitor misses the reader.
- **Benefit as value.** "Save time" is a benefit-cliché. "Cut weekly reconciliation from 8 hours to 20 minutes" is value.

---

## How to apply when rewriting

1. Before writing a line of copy, answer in notes: who's the best-for, what's the real alternative, what's the unique attribute, what value does it create, what's the category. 60 seconds.
2. Lead with **value** + **best-for**, not with the product. The reader should know who this is for in the first sentence.
3. Introduce the product against the real alternative, not against abstract "legacy tools".
4. Every attribute gets a value translation. If you can't translate it, cut it.
5. Use only attributes and proof present in the input. Don't invent customer names, percentages, or benchmarks.

## Before/after references (shape, not content to copy)

**Best-for + value lead (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "Xentral is a modern ERP for growing businesses."
- Strong: "For DTC ops leads running inventory across Shopify, Amazon, and two warehouses on spreadsheets — Xentral is the Warenwirtschaft that keeps them in sync."

**Attribute → value translation (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "Real-time multichannel inventory."
- Strong: "Real-time inventory across every channel — so you stop hearing about stockouts from customers before you do."

**Best-for narrowing (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "Ideal for mid-market ecommerce."
- Strong: "Best for DTC and B2B brands doing €5–50M, running 3+ sales channels, with an ops lead who's currently the bottleneck."

**Competitive alternative framing (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "Better than legacy ERP."
- Strong: "Most brands your size aren't choosing between ERPs — they're choosing between a spreadsheet they trust and one they're scared to touch. Xentral is the first option that isn't a spreadsheet."

**Category pick (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "Cloud ERP platform."
- Strong: "Warenwirtschaft (ERP) built for DTC — not retrofitted from finance software."
