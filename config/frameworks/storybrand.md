# StoryBrand

Apply Donald Miller's StoryBrand BrandScript. The customer is the hero. The brand is the guide — never the hero. Structure the copy so the reader sees themselves, their problem, a clear path forward, and what's at stake.

## The seven elements

### 1 — A Character (the reader)
Open on the reader, not the brand. Name who they are, what they want. One sentence. Concrete role, not a persona ("operations lead at a 40-person DTC brand", not "growing businesses").
- **Test:** Would the reader say "that's me" in the first line?

### 2 — Has a Problem
Surface three layers of problem, in order:
- **External** — the tangible, visible problem ("orders ship late").
- **Internal** — how that problem makes them feel ("I'm always firefighting, never ahead").
- **Philosophical** — why it's wrong that this problem exists ("ops people shouldn't spend their week babysitting spreadsheets").
You don't need all three in every post. But the internal one is what hooks emotion.

### 3 — Meets a Guide
The brand shows up here — as Yoda, not Luke. Show empathy ("we've run this too") and authority (proof: years, customers, outcomes). Two short sentences. Never dominate.
- **Avoid:** hero-brand language. "We're the leading…", "Our award-winning…". The reader doesn't care — they care about themselves.

### 4 — Who Gives Them a Plan
A plan removes risk. Two forms:
- **Process plan** — "Connect your shop → we import orders → you ship from one screen." 3–4 steps, max.
- **Agreement plan** — what they won't have to deal with. "No re-training. No new warehouse process. No more spreadsheet gymnastics."
The plan is what makes them feel safe enough to act.

### 5 — And Calls Them to Action
One **direct** CTA (book, start, buy) and optionally one **transitional** CTA (download, watch, read) for people not ready yet. Never end a post without a specific next step.
- **Test:** Is there exactly one primary action? Is it a verb?

### 6 — That Helps Them Avoid Failure
Name the cost of inaction. Not fearmongering — a specific, real cost. "Teams that stay on spreadsheets spend ~8 hours a week on reconciliation they'll never get back." Stakes make the CTA weigh something.

### 7 — And Ends in Success
Paint the transformation. Not features — the after-state identity. "You stop being the firefighter and start being the ops leader the business actually needs." The reader should be able to see themselves there.

---

## Anti-patterns — avoid these

- **Brand-as-hero.** Any sentence where the brand is the subject of the transformation. Rewrite so the reader is.
- **Too many problems.** Pick one external problem. Multiple problems diffuse the hook.
- **No plan.** Without 3-step clarity the reader assumes it's complicated.
- **Weak CTA.** "Learn more" is not a CTA. Use a verb that implies commitment.
- **Success = features.** "Get real-time inventory" is a feature. "Stop hearing about stockouts from customers before you do" is success.

---

## How to apply when rewriting

1. Identify the reader in one concrete sentence. If the input is brand-first, flip it.
2. Extract the strongest single problem and name the internal layer explicitly.
3. Position the brand as guide in two sentences max — empathy then authority.
4. Compress the offer into a 3-step plan. Cut everything else.
5. End with one verb-led CTA and a one-line picture of life after.
6. Only use claims present in the input. Don't invent customer counts, timelines, or outcomes.

## Before/after references (shape, not content to copy)

**Character (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "Growing businesses need better inventory tools."
- Strong: "You're running ops at a 30-person brand and inventory is always one surprise away from a stockout."

**Problem — internal layer (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "Spreadsheets are inefficient."
- Strong: "You already know the spreadsheet is wrong. You just don't have time to fix it before the next order lands."

**Guide (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "Xentral is the leading ERP for mid-sized brands."
- Strong: "We've run the same operation you're running. Over 1,800 brands have moved off spreadsheets on Xentral."

**Plan (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "Comprehensive integration and onboarding support."
- Strong: "Connect your shop. Import your orders. Ship from one screen. That's week one."

**CTA + Success (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "Learn more about how Xentral can help your business grow."
- Strong: "Book a 20-minute walkthrough. Two weeks from now you'll be running orders, not chasing them."
